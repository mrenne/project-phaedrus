Project Curio

"... [the literate] will not practice using their memory because they will put their trust in writing."

— Plato

A hub for the intersection of the tactile and the virtual. Project Curio bridges the gap between traditional physical objects—beginning with the humble paperback book—and the emergent intelligence of AI chatbots.

The First Experiment

Our first game transforms your bookshelf into a repository of living characters. By pairing a physical paperback with a custom chatbot interface, readers no longer just observe the text; they converse with it, probe its memories, and challenge its logic.

Paperback Integration

Utilizing ISBN scanning and OCR to connect your unique physical copy to our digital game engine.

Gen AI Chatbot

Google Gem AI trained on literary analysis to provide deep, meaningful interactions.

Physical Checkpoints

The game progresses only when you confirm specific physical interactions with your book.

The Silent Interrogator

Creation Toolkit

We are building a Google Gem for makers to create their own "Phaedrus-style" games. Our Google Gem handles:

  • ✓
    Page Verification: Logic to confirm a player is looking at a specific physical page.
  • ✓
    Context Injection: Automatically feeding book passages into AI prompts for consistency.
  • ✓
    Tactile Triggers: Using phone sensors (camera, accelerometer) to verify physical game actions.

Are young people reading less?

Research indicates a sharp decline in "reading for pleasure," which is a strong predictor of academic success.

  • The 40% Drop:A 2025 study from the University of Florida found that reading for fun in the U.S. has fallen by 40% over the last two decades.
  • Daily Habits: According to the National Literacy Trust’s 2025 survey, only 1 in 5 (roughly 18.7%) children and young people (ages 8–18) read daily in their free time.
  • Enjoyment Levels: Only 32.7% of young people reported enjoying reading "very much" or "quite a lot," the lowest level since tracking began in 2005.

Project Curio attempts to answer this call to action.

By layering intelligent agents over static physical text, we create a "Living Book." We use the technology Plato might have feared (digital intelligence) to force a return to the thing he loved: the deep, dialectic interrogation of ideas—centered around the physical objects that ground our history.
